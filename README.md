# Provenance Guard

A backend system that any creative-sharing platform could plug into to classify whether submitted text was written by a human or generated by AI — and, crucially, to do so **honestly**. It never forces a binary verdict, it communicates uncertainty in plain language, and it gives creators a path to appeal a classification they dispute.

> **Design principle that drives everything here:** on a writing platform, a *false positive* — flagging a human's own work as AI-generated — is the costly error. It insults the creator and erodes trust. So the system is deliberately biased to protect against it: a high bar before it says "AI," a wide "uncertain" band, and an appeal path built for exactly the cases it gets wrong.

## Architecture overview

A submission flows through five components on its way to the label a reader sees:

```
POST /submit
   │  raw text
   ├──────────────▶ Signal 1 — Groq LLM            ──▶ llm_score (0–1)
   │  raw text
   ├──────────────▶ Signal 2 — Stylometrics        ──▶ stylometric_score (0–1)
   │
   ▼  both signal scores
Confidence scoring   S = 0.7·llm + 0.3·stylo  (0.9/0.1 for very short text)
   │  combined score S + verdict
   ├──────────────▶ Transparency label   (3 variants by threshold)
   │  decision + both signal scores
   ├──────────────▶ SQLite audit log
   ▼
JSON response  { content_id, attribution, confidence, label, signals }
```

**Appeal flow:** `POST /appeal` looks up the original decision by `content_id`, flips its status to `under_review`, writes an audit-log entry that carries the *original* classification values alongside the creator's reasoning, and returns a confirmation. (A full architecture diagram with both flows lives in [`planning.md`](planning.md#architecture).)

## Detection signals

The pipeline combines **two genuinely distinct signals** — one *semantic*, one *structural*. They look at different properties of the text, so together they are more informative than either alone.

### Signal 1 — LLM classification (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)

- **What it measures:** holistic semantic and stylistic coherence. The text is sent to the model with a structured prompt that returns a 0–1 AI-likelihood plus a one-line rationale.
- **Why it differs between human and AI:** LLMs recognize the "fingerprint" of LLM prose — hedging boilerplate ("It is important to note…"), even rhythm, topic-sentence-then-elaboration structure, and an absence of lived, specific detail.
- **What it misses:** it is non-deterministic (the same text can score slightly differently), it can be fooled by lightly human-edited AI text, and it carries the model's own biases — notably a tendency to over-flag formal or non-native English as "AI-like." It is also a black box: it can't fully explain *why* it returned a score.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python)

Three measurable statistical properties, each normalized to a 0–1 "AI-likeness" sub-score (1 = statistically AI-like, i.e. uniform), then combined.

| Metric | What it captures | Human vs. AI tendency |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Sentence-length variance** ("burstiness") | how much sentence length varies | humans are bursty (short next to long); AI is uniform |
| **Type-token ratio** | vocabulary diversity (unique ÷ total words) | AI trends toward smooth, moderately-diverse vocabulary |
| **Punctuation density** | punctuation marks ÷ words | AI over-uses commas/semicolons in an even rhythm |

- **How they combine:** burstiness-weighted, *not* a flat average, because burstiness is the most defensible of the three while TTR and punctuation are noisier:
  `stylometric_score = 0.5·burstiness + 0.25·TTR + 0.25·punctuation`.
- **What it misses:** it is unreliable on **short text** (a 2-sentence input has almost no variance to measure) and it is **content-blind** — it cannot distinguish *deliberate* stylistic uniformity (a minimalist poem, a legal notice) from AI uniformity, so it will false-positive on intentionally repetitive or simple human writing.

### Why this pairing

The LLM reads *meaning*; stylometrics measures *structure*. A clever AI text might fool the structural metrics but trip the LLM's semantic radar, and vice-versa. Both individual scores are always recorded in the audit log so any verdict is fully traceable.

## Confidence scoring

Both signals produce a 0–1 score. They are combined into a single **AI-likeness score `S`**:

```
S = 0.7 · llm_score + 0.3 · stylometric_score
```

The LLM is weighted heavier because it is the stronger, semantically-aware signal; stylometrics acts as a structural sanity check. (For submissions under ~40 words the blend shifts to `0.9·llm + 0.1·stylo`, because stylometrics is just noise on text that short — see [Spec reflection](#spec-reflection).)

`S` maps to three verdicts via **deliberately asymmetric thresholds**:

| AI-likeness `S` | Verdict | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| `S ≥ 0.70` | **likely_ai** | a high bar — strong evidence required before flagging a human |
| `0.40 ≤ S < 0.70` | **uncertain** | a wide middle band; when unsure, say so |
| `S < 0.40` | **likely_human** | humans get the benefit of the doubt |

The band is not centered on 0.5 — the uncertain zone is pulled *upward* toward the AI side, so borderline text drifts toward *uncertain* or *human* rather than toward an accusation. **What `S = 0.5` means to a user: "we can't tell"** — it sits in the middle of the uncertain band.

The user-facing **confidence** reflects how sure we are of *the verdict we reached*: for `likely_ai` it's `S`; for `likely_human` it's `1 − S`; for `uncertain` no headline percentage is shown (a number there would imply a precision the system doesn't have).

### How I validated it's meaningful

I ran four deliberately-chosen inputs (clearly-AI, clearly-human, formal-human, lightly-edited-AI) and confirmed the scores **spread across all three bands** instead of clustering at 0.5 or flipping binary at a single point. When a result looked off, I printed `llm_score` and `stylometric_score` separately to see which signal was responsible — that's how I discovered the TTR-on-short-text issue documented below.

### Two example submissions (real output)

**High-confidence case — clearly AI-generated:**

```json
{
  "attribution": "likely_ai",
  "confidence": 0.7362,
  "label": "⚠️ Likely AI-generated. Our analysis found strong signs this text was produced by an AI tool (about 74% confidence)...",
  "signals": { "llm_score": 0.9, "stylometric_score": 0.354, "combined_score": 0.7362 }
}
```

**Lower-confidence case — clearly human-written:**

```json
{
  "attribution": "likely_human",
  "confidence": 0.7997,
  "label": "✓ Likely human-written. This text reads as human-authored (about 80% confidence)...",
  "signals": { "llm_score": 0.2, "stylometric_score": 0.201, "combined_score": 0.2003 }
}
```

A third, genuinely borderline input (a formal paragraph on monetary policy) produced `combined_score = 0.6301 → uncertain` — landing in the middle band rather than being wrongly flagged as AI. That gap between **0.20, 0.63, and 0.74** is the meaningful variation: the score is not a constant, and it doesn't binary-flip.

## Transparency label

The label is what a non-technical reader sees. It uses plain language (no "classifier," no "score"), and the text **changes by verdict** — not just a number. The AI and human variants embed the confidence as a percentage; the uncertain variant deliberately carries no number.

**Variant A — high-confidence AI (`likely_ai`):**

> ⚠️ Likely AI-generated. Our analysis found strong signs this text was produced by an AI tool (about 74% confidence). Automated detection isn't perfect — if you wrote this yourself, you can appeal this result.

**Variant B — high-confidence human (`likely_human`):**

> ✓ Likely human-written. This text reads as human-authored (about 80% confidence). We found no strong signs of AI generation.

**Variant C — uncertain (`uncertain`):**

> ❓ Inconclusive. We couldn't determine with confidence whether this text was written by a person or an AI tool. Please treat this result as uncertain — it should not be taken as a judgment either way.

Only the **AI** label invites an appeal. That asymmetry is intentional: the AI verdict is the one that can harm a creator, so it carries the remedy.

## Appeals workflow

Any creator holding a `content_id` can contest their classification by sending their reasoning to `POST /appeal`. The system:

1. looks up the original decision by `content_id`,
2. updates its status from `classified` → `under_review`,
3. writes an audit-log entry capturing the appeal **alongside the original decision** (the original attribution, confidence, and both signal scores, plus the new `creator_reasoning`), and
4. returns a confirmation.

There is no automated re-classification by design — an appeal flags content for a human reviewer and stops there. A reviewer opening the queue would see every `under_review` record with its original verdict, both signal scores, the timestamp, and the creator's stated reasoning.

```json
// POST /appeal response
{
  "content_id": "f4a7eb14-6489-46bb-a4e4-332558210f42",
  "status": "under_review",
  "message": "Your appeal has been received. This content is now under review by a human moderator."
}
```

## Rate limiting

`POST /submit` is rate-limited with **Flask-Limiter** (in-memory storage) to:

```
5 per minute;50 per day   (per IP address)
```

**Reasoning — tied to realistic writing-platform usage:**

- **5 per minute.** A real creator submits their *own* work — a handful of pieces, occasionally bursty when revising and resubmitting a draft. Five per minute comfortably covers that without ever blocking a legitimate writer, while a script trying to probe the detector hits the wall almost immediately.
- **50 per day.** No honest creator submits 50 distinct pieces in a day; that volume only looks like scraping or flooding. The daily cap stops a slow-drip abuser who stays under the per-minute limit.
- **Bonus:** every `/submit` calls the Groq API, so these limits also protect our upstream cost and quota from being drained by abuse.

These are intentionally tighter than the tutorial's default (10/min, 100/day) because a single-creator workflow simply doesn't need that headroom, and the lower ceiling is cheaper to defend.

**Evidence — 12 rapid requests against the 5/min limit:**

```
request  1 -> 200      request  7 -> 429
request  2 -> 200      request  8 -> 429
request  3 -> 200      request  9 -> 429
request  4 -> 200      request 10 -> 429
request  5 -> 200      request 11 -> 429
request  6 -> 429      request 12 -> 429
```

A blocked request returns a structured `429` body, not Flask's default HTML error page:

```json
{ "error": "rate_limit_exceeded",
  "message": "Too many submissions. Please slow down and try again later.",
  "limit": "5 per 1 minute" }
```

## Known limitations

**The system will misclassify a non-native English speaker's formal writing as AI-like — and this is tied directly to both signals.** Careful, flat, low-burstiness prose with even punctuation is exactly what the stylometric signal reads as "uniform = AI," *and* the LLM itself carries a known bias toward flagging formal or non-native phrasing. So both signals push the same wrong direction, and the error compounds. In testing, a formal human paragraph scored `0.63` — saved from a false `likely_ai` only by the deliberately high 0.70 threshold, landing it in `uncertain` instead. This is the precise scenario the appeal path exists for, and the appeal example in the audit log above is exactly this case.

A second, related weakness surfaced during scoring validation: **the type-token ratio metric is unreliable on short text.** TTR runs mechanically high (≈0.85–0.90) on inputs under ~50 words, so it never clears its AI-likeness threshold and contributes ≈0 to the stylometric score on short submissions — leaving burstiness and punctuation to carry the structural signal alone. A length-normalized measure (e.g. a moving-average TTR over a fixed window) would fix this; I left it out to keep the heuristics simple and honest about their range.

## Spec reflection

**One way the spec helped.** Writing out the three label variants *verbatim in `planning.md` before any code* forced me to decide what `S = 0.5` should mean to a user ("we can't tell") before I wrote the scoring function — not after. Because the target text already existed, the label generator was a near-mechanical translation of the spec, and I could confirm the implementation matched word-for-word.

**One way the implementation diverged.** `planning.md` originally specified the stylometric metrics would be combined by a flat average and said nothing about short text. During Milestone 4 validation, two things changed. First, I switched to a **burstiness-weighted blend (0.5/0.25/0.25)** after seeing that TTR and punctuation were noisier than burstiness on the test inputs. Second — and not anticipated in the plan at all — I added a **short-text rule**: under ~40 words the blend shifts to `0.9·llm + 0.1·stylo`, because a one- or two-sentence input gives stylometrics no variance to measure and its score becomes noise. I updated `planning.md` to record both changes rather than letting the spec drift out of sync with the code.

## AI usage

I used Claude (via Claude Code) as a pair-programmer throughout, always feeding it sections of `planning.md` as the spec. Two concrete instances:

**1. Generating the stylometric signal and confidence-scoring logic (M4).** I directed Claude to implement Signal 2 and the score-combination function from `planning.md` §1–§2. It produced working code that combined the three stylometric metrics with an **equal-weight average** — faithfully matching what my plan literally said ("averaged"). I **overrode** that: after testing, I changed it to a burstiness-weighted blend because burstiness was the most reliable metric and an equal average let the two noisier metrics drag the result. I also directed the addition of the short-text down-weighting rule, which neither the plan nor Claude's first draft included.

**2. Generating the label function and `/appeal` endpoint (M5).** I asked Claude to generate `generate_label()` and the appeal route from `planning.md` §3–§4. I **verified the label text against the spec verbatim** and confirmed all three variants were reachable. For the appeal endpoint, Claude's natural implementation would have stored only the new appeal data; I **decided differently** — the appeal log entry preserves the *original* classification's scores and verdict so the log literally shows the contested decision beside the appeal, which is what makes the audit trail meaningful (and what the rubric asks for). I also added the 404 path for unknown `content_id`s, which the first draft didn't handle.
